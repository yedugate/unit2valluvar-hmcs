🍎 Food Standards

1. History of Food Standards

  • Pre-modern era: Rules existed in ancient texts (e.g., Manusmriti, Sangam literature) warning against impure/adulterated food.
  • 19th–20th century: Many countries introduced laws to prevent adulteration (e.g., UK’s Adulteration of Food Act, 1860).
  • India: Prevention of Food Adulteration Act (PFA), 1954 → later replaced by Food Safety and Standards Act (FSSA), 2006.
  • Global: Codex Alimentarius Commission established in 1963 by FAO & WHO to set international food standards.

2. What are Food Standards?

Definition: Food standards are official rules, regulations, and guidelines that ensure food is safe, hygienic, nutritious, and suitable for human consumption. They cover the entire food chain — farm → processing → retail → consumer.

7. Top 10 Key Terms

  1. Food Standard – Official rule for food quality & safety
  2. Adulteration – Mixing inferior/unsafe substances in food
  3. Contaminant – Harmful chemical, toxin, or microbe in food
  4. Additive – Substance added to preserve or improve food
  5. Labeling – Mandatory consumer information on pack
  6. Packaging – Safe container to protect food quality
  7. FSSAI – India’s statutory body for food safety
  8. BIS – Bureau setting product specifications
  9. Codex Alimentarius – International food standards body
  10. Food Safety & Standards Act (2006) – India’s main food law

Q1. Define food standards. Explain their objectives and pillars with examples. (10 marks)

Answer:

  • Definition: Food standards are official specifications that define a food’s identity (what it is), quality (minimum criteria), and safety (limits for hazards) to protect health and ensure fair trade.
  • Objectives: Protect consumers, prevent fraud, enable fair trade, and support public health policy.
PillarWhat it setsSimple example

QualityMinimum composition, sensory criteriaMin. fat/SNF for toned milk
SafetyLimits for additives, contaminants, microbesPermitted colors only; aflatoxin limit in nuts
LabellingMandatory pack informationIngredients list, allergens, veg/non‑veg mark

Q2. How are standards developed and enforced? Illustrate the cycle and list key authorities. (10 marks)

Answer:

  • Authorities (examples): National regulator (e.g., FSSAI), international reference (Codex), state/local food safety departments, notified labs.
  • Process: Risk assessment → Draft standard → Stakeholder consultation → Notification → Implementation → Surveillance → Review.

Q4. Summarise standards for additives & contaminants with examples. Include a table. (10 marks)

Answer:

  • Standards specify which additives are permitted in a food and maximum levels (often in ppm), plus limits for contaminants (pesticide residues, heavy metals, natural toxins).
  • Always declare additives by name or INS code on labels; choose the minimum effective level (hurdle approach).
CategoryPurposeTypical classroom example*
PreservativesInhibit microbesBeverage: sodium benzoate (INS 211) — limited use
AntioxidantsPrevent ranciditySnacks: BHA/BHT — within permitted range
ColorsRestore/standardise colorOnly permitted colors; natural turmeric/annatto common
Heavy metalsSafety limitsLead/arsenic limits in drinking water & foods

Q5. Describe sampling, testing and conformity assessment under food standards. Add a flow diagram. (10 marks)

Answer:

  1. Sampling: Official sample drawn, sealed, coded; chain‑of‑custody maintained.
  2. Testing: Notified lab analyses against standard methods (composition, additives, contaminants, microbes).
  3. Assessment: Result compared with limits → compliant/non‑compliant; actions: improvement notice, recall, prosecution as applicable.
  4. Records: Labels, batches, and test reports recorded for traceability.
