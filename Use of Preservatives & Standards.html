1. What are Preservatives?

  • Definition: Preservatives are substances added to food to prevent spoilage caused by microorganisms (bacteria, yeasts, molds) or chemical changes (oxidation, rancidity).
  • Goal: Extend shelf life, maintain safety, and preserve nutritional & sensory qualities.

2. Why Are Preservatives Used?

  • Prevent microbial growth (stop food poisoning).
  • Extend storage time for distribution and trade.
  • Maintain color, flavor, and texture.
  • Reduce food wastage.

3. Classification of Preservatives

Type Function Examples

5. Standards & Regulations

  • India (FSSAI):
    • Food Safety and Standards (Food Products Standards & Food Additives) Regulations, 2011.
    • Preservatives only allowed within maximum permissible limits (ppm = parts per million).
    • All chemical preservatives must be declared on labels (name or INS code).
  • International (Codex Alimentarius):
    • Sets maximum allowable levels for each preservative across product categories.
    • Facilitates safety and global food trade.

7. Advantages & Concerns

Advantages

  • Extend shelf life.
  • Prevent microbial spoilage.
  • Maintain quality for storage/transport.

Concerns

  • Overuse may cause allergies (e.g., sulfites ‚Üí asthma).
  • Nitrites can form nitrosamines (carcinogenic) under certain conditions.
  • Strict limits and labelling are essential for safety.

Q5. Describe the process of canning and its principle.

Answer:

  • Principle: Food is placed in airtight containers, heated to destroy microbes, then sealed ‚Üí vacuum prevents recontamination.
  • Steps:
    1. Cleaning & cutting food.
    2. Filling into cans with syrup/brine.
    3. Exhausting air & sealing.
    4. Heating (sterilization at 115‚Äì121 ¬∞C).
    5. Cooling & labeling.
  • Examples: Canned fruits, vegetables, soups, jams.
  • Advantage: Long shelf life. Limitation: Vitamin loss, cost.
