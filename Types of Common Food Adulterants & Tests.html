1) Big Picture (Why this topic matters)

Food adulteration = adding/removing/substituting substances so that food quality is lowered or misleading. It causes health risks (toxicity, allergies), economic loss (cheating), and legal issues.

2) Key Definitions (Exam‚Äëfriendly)

  • Adulterant: Any substance added to food that reduces quality or is harmful/misleading.
  • Intentional adulteration: Done to gain profit (e.g., mixing cheap powders in spices).
  • Incidental adulteration: Enters accidentally (e.g., pesticide residues, dust, insects).
  • Contaminant: Unwanted substance present beyond safe limits (e.g., heavy metals).
  • Substitution: Replacing a valuable ingredient with a cheaper look‚Äëalike.
  • Dilution: Adding a cheaper material/solvent (e.g., water in milk, syrups in honey).
  • Adulterant examples: Water, starch, artificial colors, chalk, brick powder, argemone oil, mineral oil, urea (industrial), soapstone, husk, papaya seeds, metanil yellow, etc.

10) Health Hazards (short notes)

  • Synthetic dyes (e.g., metanil yellow): allergies, potential toxicity.
  • Argemone oil: serious poisoning (epidemic dropsy).
  • Mineral oil in edible oils: digestive/long‚Äëterm risks.
  • Kesari dhal (Lathyrus): risk of neurolathyrism on long‚Äëterm high intake.
  • Detergents in milk: GI irritation.
  • Chalk/sand: GI irritation; reduces nutrition.

11) Consumer Safety & Good Practices

  • Buy from reliable sources; check label, batch, date.
  • Prefer whole spices (grind at home) vs pre‚Äëpowdered.
  • Store dry goods airtight; keep away from moisture.
  • For suspicious samples, don‚Äôt consume; send to a certified lab.
  • Keep receipts; note batch details for any complaint.
