1) Big Picture

  • Food adulteration = adding, removing, or substituting substances so the food‚Äôs quality is lowered or misleading (e.g., adding water to milk, artificial dyes to spices).
  • Food preservation = slowing spoilage by controlling microbes, enzymes, oxygen, moisture, temperature, and time using safe methods (cold/heat/chemicals/packaging/irradiation, etc.).
  • Together they protect public health: avoid adulteration (fraud/contamination) and apply preservation (safe shelf life).

3) Food Adulteration ‚Äî Core Notes

A) Types (with examples)

Type What it means Example(s)
Intentional Deliberately added cheap/unsafe substances Water in milk, brick powder in chilli, argemone oil in mustard oil
Incidental Accidental contamination from environment/process Dust, metal filings, pesticide residues, storage pests
Substitution Replacing a costly ingredient with a cheaper one Chicory for coffee, kesari dal mixed with pulses
Concealment of damage Using colors/flavors to hide spoilage Artificial dyes to make stale turmeric look fresh

B) Why adulteration happens

  • Profit motive, poor regulation awareness, weak supply chain checks, and lack of consumer vigilance.

C) Simple Household Screening Tests (Awareness)

Indicative only; lab confirmation needed for legal action.

  • Starch in milk: Cool milk + iodine ‚Üí blue = starch present.
  • Detergent in milk: Shake with water ‚Üí persistent foam.
  • Artificial color in spices: Rub on wet cotton ‚Üí color streaks.
  • Chalk in sugar: Add to water; chalk settles/effervescence with acid.
  • Argemone oil in mustard oil: Nitric acid ‚Üí reddish‚Äëbrown tint (screen).

D) Effects of adulteration

  • Health risks (allergies, organ damage if toxic), nutrient loss, consumer fraud, loss of trust.

5) Food Additives vs Preservatives (Quick Clarity)

Term Purpose Examples
Food additive Any permitted substance added for technology function (stabilize, color, sweeten, preserve, etc.) Emulsifiers, colors, antioxidants, preservatives
Preservative Type of additive specifically to inhibit spoilage Class I (salt, sugar, oil, vinegar) ‚Ä¢ Class II (benzoate, sorbate, nitrite, sulfite)

Good practice: Use only permitted additives within limits, declare on labels (name/INS), and pair with other hurdles (pH, aw, packaging) to minimize chemical load.

8) Top 10 Key Terms (must‚Äëremember)

  1. Adulteration ‚Äì lowering quality by addition/substitution/removal
  2. Incidental contamination ‚Äì unintentional contamination from process/storage
  3. Screening test ‚Äì quick check; requires lab confirmation
  4. Food additive ‚Äì permitted substance for technological function
  5. Preservative ‚Äì additive that inhibits spoilage (Class I/II)
  6. Water activity (aw) ‚Äì ‚Äúfree water‚Äù available for microbes
  7. Pasteurization ‚Äì mild heat to reduce pathogens/enzymes
  8. Commercial sterility ‚Äì condition after canning/retort where spoilage microbes cannot grow under normal storage
  9. Modified Atmosphere Packaging (MAP) ‚Äì changing gas mix around food
  10. Hurdle technology ‚Äì combining several mild barriers for safety and shelf life

10) ‚ÄúDon‚Äôt Miss‚Äù Concepts (often left out)

  • Label literacy: declaring additive class + specific name/INS; vegetarian/non‚Äëvegetarian logo; allergen info.
  • Process validation: time‚Äìtemperature charts, pH/aw checks, packaging integrity tests.
  • Cold chain: temperature abuse = quick spoilage even after pasteurization.
  • Enzymatic spoilage (not just microbial): browning, off‚Äëflavors‚Äîneed blanching/antioxidants.
  • Packaging barriers: oxygen/moisture/light sensitivity; choose high‚Äëbarrier pouches/cans/glass as needed.
  • Storage pests & sanitation: monitoring, cleaning, FIFO/FEFO.
  • Consumer role: buy sealed packs, check dates, avoid unnaturally bright colors.
  • Nutrition vs safety: preservation should retain nutrients where possible (e.g., blanch then freeze veg).
  • Waste reduction: preservation extends shelf life ‚Üí reduces food waste.
  • Ethics & law: penalties deter intentional adulteration; labs confirm with valid methods.
